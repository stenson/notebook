沙茶博洛尼亚肉酱

sa-de bolognese

shāchá bóluòníyă ròujiàng

Sa-de aka satay aka shacha is an enchanting accompaniment to Teoswa dishes, with precise origins as murky as the “sand tea” sauce itself. Not the peanut-based dip we enjoy with our Southeast Asian meat skewers, this Teoswa sa-de is darker, oilier, and funkier thanks to dried seafood, chilis, and shallots. Origin myths include the introduction of a similar sauce by overseas Teoswa returning to their hometown, and subsequent adaptation of the sa-de to local tastes.  You may find it as a dipping sauce option at a Chinese hotpot restaurant, or as a tableside condiment if you are lucky to live close to a Teoswa noodle soup restaurant. The most popular brand of sa-de sauce is Bull Head (of Taiwan), which labels its silver cans “BBQ Sauce.” As this misnomer might suggest, sa-de is often served alongside meats, especially beef.

The sauce’s deep savoriness and common pairing with beef made me wonder how it would fare in pasta Bolognese, one of the world’s great comfort foods. Turns out the two harmonize extremely well. The resulting sauce looks and initially tastes like a typical ragù Bolognese, but then you feel a slight tingle on your tongue from the chilis and Sichuan pepper, and the hints of coconut and dried shrimp in the sa-de beckon your mind to warmer climes.

A can of Bullhead Sa-de sauce from Taiwan

I recommend using fresh flat rice noodles, which can be found unrefrigerated in well-trafficked Asian supermarkets. 2-lb. packs are common, and will feed around 3-4, depending on how hungry your guests are. The lightness of the slippery rice noodles are a lovely contrast to the substantial ragù, and the flurry of fresh herbs to finish helps balance the sauce’s deeper flavors. If you can’t find fresh rice noodles, substitute dried rice noodles or pasta, depending on personal preference (or mood!).

Chinese celery is more intensely flavored than Western celery, and the leaves are used in addition to the stalks. The stalks are often sold with a fair amount of trapped grit — make sure to wash thoroughly! (I trim the ends, cut the long stalks into sections, and swish the stalks and leaves in a large bowl of water at least twice.) Western celery is a perfectly fine substitution. Use more or less based on your fondness for the flavor (I use a quite a bit).

2 tbsp cooking oil

3 shallots, minced

3 cloves garlic, minced

A few stalks of Chinese celery, finely chopped (including leaves)

1 carrot, diced

3 tbsp or more sa-de sauce, to taste

1 lb ground beef

Shaoxing rice wine

Fish sauce

A few leaves of basil (sweet or Thai, your preference)

1 28-oz can whole tomatoes

Salt

White pepper

1 2-lb. pack of fresh flat rice noodles

Cilantro, chopped

Basil, chiffonade

Mint, chiffonade

Heat oil in large Dutch oven or pot over medium heat.

Add shallots and garlic. Sauté until shallots are translucent.

Add celery and carrot. Sauté a few minutes until they begin to soften.

Add sa-de sauce, making sure to evenly coat the aromatics.

Turn heat up to medium-high and add ground beef, breaking up any chunks. Allow the beef to sear, adding salt and white pepper. Sauté until mostly cooked through.

Add dash of Shaoxing rice wine, a few dashes of fish sauce, and basil leaves, stirring to incorporate.

Add tomatoes, using wooden spoon or spatula to begin mashing them down in the Dutch oven.

Reduce heat to low and let simmer for at least 45 minutes (longer is better), stirring occasionally.

Shortly before serving, bring pot of water to boil for noodles.

For fresh rice noodles, a quick dip is sufficient for softening and separating the strands. Use chopsticks to loosen any noodle clumps in the water, and use slotted spoon or mesh strainer to quickly transfer noodles to serving bowls. For dried noodles, follow package instructions.

Taste sauce and add salt, pepper, fish sauce, or sa-de to taste.

Serve noodles with a ladle of sauce on top. Garnish with cilantro, basil, and mint.

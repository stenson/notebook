Dried Honey Dates

Something plump for a little unctuousness — January 25, 2016

The only detail listed for the “honey” cultivar on the “date palm” Wikipedia page is “unknown.” Hmm. Intriguing!

I was introduced to dried honey dates only a few years ago, while searching for ways to oomph a basic pork broth, the springboard for many Chinese soups. Over the years, I had brewed many batches of underwhelming pork infusions. Clearly my technique was off. The first culprit I identified was my pork bone/scraps:water ratio. I really wanted to get more out of each potful of broth, but it was very obviously backfiring.

Lesson #1: don't push your ingredients to strive! As with certain people, asking a pork bone to give 110% is just not motivational--and only leaves you crying into a sad bowl of weak pork-water.

But even after reducing my water levels, something was missing. I always threw in some basic aromatics — ginger, scallions, maybe some peppercorns or other spices--but there was no depth to my pork broth's character. A Google search returned a few ideas, including adding in the mellifluously named "honey date." In the Asian grocery, I found these dried fruits packaged about 20 to a bag in the Chinese herb aisle, and also saw a few included in various soup-mix herb packs.

They have a satisfying heft to them (unlike the more ubiquitous red dried dates, which are dainty things) and are covered in a fine film of sugar crust. A sniff reveals a turnip-y sulfurous-y undertone, which mellows quite nicely in the pot and provides some much-needed bass to an otherwise one-dimensional broth. Brewed by itself, it has a bit of a mushroomy flavor.

Lesson #2: a little stank is your friend, my friend.

It appears that the honey date's name refers to its preparation method rather than its cultivar. One of the Chinese herb & spice guides on my shelf — Terry Tan’s Little Guide Book — introduces honey dates simply as a “third variety”; red and black dates are one and two. In Grace Young’s Wisdom of the Chinese Kitchen, they also go by “brown dates.” But, most informatively, Teresa M. Chen’s A Tradition of Soup says it's just a jujube that's been preserved in honey. Mystery solved — thanks, Teresa!

Pork Broth

When making a pork broth, I always parboil the bones first. While this step may seem a bother, it saves you the pain of skimming later on, and rewards you with a beautifully clear broth.

  • •Bring a pot of water to boil & add the pork bones. I usually use at least 2 lbs of bones (really bones & meat) — if I’m making broth, I might as well make a big batch! Meaty neck bones do well here, as do ribs.
  • •Bring back up to a boil and let boil for about 5 minutes. This allows the bones to release their gunky bits.
  • •Pluck out the bones and give them a rinse. Discard the parboiling water.
  • •Return the bones to the pot with enough cold water to cover by an inch or so.1
  • •Along with a rinsed honey date, I like throwing in a few scallions and a smashed piece of ginger to round out the flavors.
  • •Bring the whole thing to a boil, then reduce the heat and simmer on low for at least an hour (preferably longer).
  • •From here, you can add a range of vegetable complements, like cubed winter melon, carrots, daikon...Let vegetables cook until tender.
  • •Salt to taste before serving, and scatter with some chopped cilantro and a dash of white pepper if you’d like. Enjoy!
